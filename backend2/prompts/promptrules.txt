============================================================
PROMPT ENGINEERING RULES — for designing system prompts
============================================================

Sourced from official Anthropic and OpenAI guidance, last
verified 2026-05-25 against current docs.

Source tags per rule:
  [A] = Anthropic Prompting Best Practices
  [O] = OpenAI Prompt Engineering Guide
  [E] = Engineering hygiene — widely practiced in the
        prompt-engineering community; not in official guides
        from either company.

============================================================
SECTION 1 — FOUNDATIONS
============================================================

1. DEFINE SUCCESS CRITERIA BEFORE YOU WRITE [A]
   Decide what the prompt MUST do before drafting it. Write
   2-3 testable conditions and 10-20 representative inputs.
   Without this, you cannot tell if iteration is progress or
   random wandering.
   Source: Anthropic — "A clear definition of the success
   criteria for your use case... some ways to empirically
   test against those criteria."

2. ROLE & IDENTITY FIRST [A, O]
   Open with one sentence stating WHO the model is — role,
   expertise level, frame of reference. Even a single
   sentence shifts tone and behavior.
   Source: Anthropic — "Setting a role in the system prompt
   focuses Claude's behavior and tone for your use case.
   Even a single sentence makes a difference."
   Example: "You are a senior tax accountant with 15 years
   of US corporate tax experience."

3. STATE THE OBJECTIVE EXPLICITLY [O]
   What is the model trying to accomplish? Place near the
   top, before any constraints.
   Source: OpenAI GPT-5.x skeleton:
   role → personality → goal → success criteria →
   constraints → output.

4. BE SPECIFIC AND DETAILED [A, O]
   "Reply in 3 sentences" beats "be concise." Every vague
   adjective is interpreted by the model — replace it with
   a measurable rule.
   Source: Anthropic — "Show your prompt to a colleague
   with minimal context. If they'd be confused, Claude
   will be too."
   OpenAI — "The more specific and detailed your
   instructions, the more likely you'll receive the output
   you want."

5. EXPLAIN WHY — INCLUDE MOTIVATION [A]
   When you give an instruction, include the reason. The
   model uses the WHY to handle edge cases the rule didn't
   anticipate.
   Source: Anthropic — "Providing context or motivation
   behind your instructions, such as explaining to Claude
   why such behavior is important, can help Claude better
   understand your goals."
   Example: "Never use ellipses → Your response will be
   read aloud by a text-to-speech engine, so never use
   ellipses."

============================================================
SECTION 2 — STRUCTURE
============================================================

6. USE XML TAGS OR DELIMITERS TO SEPARATE SECTIONS [A, O]
   Wrap each block of your prompt — instructions, context,
   examples, user data — in its own tag or delimiter.
   Helps the model parse complex prompts AND prevents it
   from treating user input as instructions.
   Source: Anthropic — "XML tags help Claude parse complex
   prompts unambiguously, especially when your prompt mixes
   instructions, context, examples, and variable inputs."
   OpenAI — "Use delimiters like markdown, XML tags, and
   section titles to clearly indicate distinct parts."

7. LONG-CONTEXT: DOCS AT TOP, QUERY AT BOTTOM [A]
   For prompts with long documents, place documents FIRST
   and the actual query LAST. Anthropic reports up to 30%
   better quality with this ordering. The query at the end
   keeps the model's attention on what you actually want.
   Source: Anthropic — "Place your long documents and
   inputs near the top of your prompt, above your query,
   instructions, and examples... improve response quality
   by up to 30%."

8. NUMBERED STEPS WHEN ORDER MATTERS [A]
   When sequence is important, use a numbered list or
   bullets. Prose buries the order.
   Source: Anthropic — "Provide instructions as sequential
   steps using numbered lists or bullet points when the
   order or completeness of steps matters."

9. STATE SCOPE EXPLICITLY [A]
   Newer Claude models (4.7+) are literal. They will not
   silently generalize an instruction from one item to
   another. If the rule applies broadly, say so.
   Source: Anthropic — "Apply this formatting to every
   section, not just the first one" (for Claude 4.7).

============================================================
SECTION 3 — WRITING STYLE
============================================================

10. TELL IT WHAT TO DO — NOT WHAT NOT TO DO [A]
    "Respond in formal English" beats "don't use slang."
    Positive instructions give a clear target.
    Source: Anthropic — "Tell Claude what to do instead of
    what not to do. Instead of: 'Do not use markdown' /
    Try: 'Your response should be composed of smoothly
    flowing prose paragraphs.'"

11. DIAL BACK AGGRESSIVE LANGUAGE FOR NEWER MODELS [A]
    Claude 4.5+ over-triggers on "CRITICAL:", "YOU MUST",
    all-caps demands, threats. Use normal English.
    Source: Anthropic — "Where you might have said
    'CRITICAL: You MUST use this tool when...' you can use
    more normal prompting."

12. SET TONE & VOICE EXPLICITLY [A, O]
    Pick a register: warm, plain, formal, witty. The model
    defaults to a generic voice if you don't.
    Source: OpenAI — "Personality and style work best when
    you define a clear agent persona."
    Anthropic — model upgrades can shift default tone;
    "re-evaluate style prompts against the new baseline."

============================================================
SECTION 4 — EXAMPLES & REASONING
============================================================

13. INCLUDE 3-5 EXAMPLES, COVER EDGE CASES [A]
    For non-trivial format or tone, include 3-5 input /
    output examples. Make them diverse — include edge
    cases — so the model doesn't lock onto a narrow pattern.
    Source: Anthropic — "Include 3-5 examples for best
    results... Diverse: Cover edge cases and vary enough
    that Claude doesn't pick up unintended patterns."

14. EXTENDED THINKING — USE THE API, NOT THE WORD "THINK" [A]
    For modern Claude models (4.5+), enable extended
    thinking via the API parameter, not prompt-level tags.
    When extended thinking is OFF, also avoid the word
    "think" in your prompt — Claude 4.5+ tries to engage
    thinking on that word even when disabled. Use
    "consider," "evaluate," or "reason through" instead.
    Manual <thinking> tags are only a fallback when API
    thinking is unavailable.
    Source: Anthropic — "Consider moving to adaptive
    thinking... Claude Opus 4.5 is particularly sensitive
    to the word 'think'... consider alternatives like
    'consider,' 'evaluate,' or 'reason through.'"

15. GROUND LONG-DOC RESPONSES IN QUOTES [A]
    For long-document tasks, ask the model to first quote
    the relevant parts of the documents before answering.
    Cuts through noise and reduces hallucination.
    Source: Anthropic — "Ask Claude to quote relevant parts
    of the documents first before carrying out its task."

============================================================
SECTION 5 — OUTPUT CONTROL
============================================================

16. DEFINE THE OUTPUT FORMAT EXACTLY [A, O]
    Specify structure, length, tone, required fields. For
    JSON, give a sample shape. Never leave the model to
    guess.
    Source: OpenAI — "Output only the requested format. Do
    not add prose or markdown fences unless requested."

17. USE STRUCTURED OUTPUTS / TOOL CALLS FOR SCHEMAS [A]
    For machine-parseable JSON, use the Structured Outputs
    feature or a tool call — NOT prefill. Prefilling the
    assistant turn with `{` is unsupported on Claude 4.6+
    (returns 400 error). Either use Structured Outputs, or
    instruct directly: "Respond with raw JSON; no preamble."
    Source: Anthropic — "Starting with Claude 4.6 models...
    prefilled responses on the last assistant turn are no
    longer supported. The Structured Outputs feature is
    designed specifically to constrain Claude's responses
    to follow a given schema."

============================================================
SECTION 6 — ITERATION & CHAINING
============================================================

18. ITERATE AGAINST A REPRESENTATIVE SAMPLE [A, O]
    Build 10-20 test inputs covering normal + edge cases.
    Run them on each prompt change. Don't trust a prompt
    until it passes the eval set, not just your own
    judgment.
    Source: Anthropic — "We recommend iterating on prompts
    against a subset of your evals or test cases."
    OpenAI — "Test systematically: Evaluate prompt changes
    against a representative sample."

19. CHAIN PROMPTS FOR COMPLEX TASKS [A]
    For complex workflows, use prompt chaining: generate a
    draft → have the model review it against criteria →
    refine based on the review. Don't try to do it in one
    shot.
    Source: Anthropic — "The most common chaining pattern
    is self-correction: generate a draft → review against
    criteria → refine based on the review."

============================================================
SECTION 7 — ENGINEERING HYGIENE
============================================================
These are widely-practiced rules from the prompt-eng
community. Useful but NOT in Anthropic's or OpenAI's
official guides — treat as engineering best practice.

20. AUDIT FOR CONTRADICTIONS [E]
    Don't say "be concise" and "thoroughly explain your
    reasoning" in the same prompt. The model resolves
    conflicts randomly — and usually against you.

21. DEFINE FALLBACK BEHAVIOR FOR MISSING INFO [E]
    What does the model do when it doesn't know? Pick ONE:
    say "I don't have that information," ask a clarifying
    question, or refuse. An undefined fallback becomes a
    hallucination.

22. VERSION YOUR PROMPTS [E]
    Track changes the way you track code. Small wording
    tweaks shift behavior; without a version log you
    cannot debug regressions.

23. WRITE MODEL-SPECIFIC, DON'T ASSUME PORTABILITY [E, A]
    Anthropic publishes per-model prompting guidance because
    each generation behaves differently. A prompt tuned for
    Claude 4.7 may not work on 4.6 or Haiku. Older patterns
    (prefill, "CRITICAL:" framing, <thinking> tags) fail or
    misfire on newer models. Either target one model, or
    test on each one you support.
